How it works

One bead, three colours, one clear answer.

Anthocyanin pigment from butterfly pea flowers is a natural pH indicator. As bacteria break food down, they release ammonia and volatile amines that raise the surrounding pH — and the pigment answers by shifting colour, right on the packaging.

Natural pigment

Sourced from butterfly pea flowers (Clitoria ternatea), a plant already used in food and drinks across the region.

Alginate beads

The pigment is trapped inside small alginate gel beads, keeping it stable, visible and easy to attach to packaging.

Readable at a glance

No app, no meter — a colour anyone can read, from a home kitchen to a school science fair.

Safety precautions

  • Wear gloves when handling raw food samples.
  • Do not taste or consume food used in this experiment.
  • Wash hands after handling spoiled samples.
  • Dispose of samples in the sealed bags provided.
  • Adult supervision recommended for primary school users.
  • Keep beads away from eyes; rinse with water on contact.

Scientific Analysis

The chemistry and biology behind every colour change — from bacterial growth to the pigment that reveals it.

What causes food spoilage

Spoilage begins when bacteria naturally present on food start breaking down proteins and fats. As they multiply, they produce by-products — including ammonia and volatile amines — that change the chemistry of the food's surface.

Role of bacteria

Species such as Pseudomonas and Enterobacteriaceae thrive on proteins in meat, fish and shrimp, converting them into nitrogen-based compounds as they grow.

Ammonia, amines & pH

These volatile amines are alkaline. As they accumulate, the pH around the food rises from a near-neutral or slightly acidic starting point toward the alkaline range — the exact shift the indicator is built to detect.

Anthocyanin chemistry

Anthocyanins change molecular structure at different pH levels, which changes which wavelengths of light they absorb — and therefore what colour we see.

Butterfly pea flower as a pH indicator

Butterfly pea (Clitoria ternatea) is rich in delphinidin-based anthocyanins. It has long been used as a natural food colourant — the same sensitivity to pH that makes it useful in cooking makes it a reliable freshness sensor.

Alginate encapsulation & smart packaging

Dropping the pigment extract into a calcium chloride bath forms small alginate gel beads through ionic gelation, locking the pigment in place. Attached to packaging film, this is a simple example of "smart" or "intelligent" packaging that communicates the condition of the food inside.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the indicator bead safe to touch?

Yes — it's made from food-grade alginate and plant pigment, but it should not be eaten as part of the experiment.

How long does one bead last?

Typically up to 48 hours once exposed to air, depending on humidity and storage.

Can this replace expiry dates?

No — it's an educational and supplementary tool, not a certified food-safety device.

What age group is this suitable for?

Designed for primary and secondary school students, with adult supervision for younger learners.
